37: ABSTRACT
38: The escalating volume of space debris in Low Earth Orbit (LEO) introduces significant collision risks to critical satellite missions and global communication infrastructure. Each new piece of debris heightens the probability of catastrophic collisions, threatening operational assets. Traditional collision avoidance strategies typically rely on fixed thresholds and manual interventions, which lack the adaptability needed for dynamic LEO conditions. These conventional methods often result in inefficient fuel consumption and delayed responses, forcing operators into a constant trade-off between preserving limited fuel and the imperative of avoiding collisions. To address these limitations, this project introduces a Reinforcement Learning (RL) based decision-support system designed for autonomous satellite collision avoidance maneuver selection. The core methodology leverages the principles of Markov Decision Processes (MDP) to enable sequential decision-making under uncertainty. Conjunction events are comprehensively modeled using key state parameters, including collision probability, miss distance, relative velocity, and available fuel. The RL agent selects from available responses—ranging from no maneuver to large maneuvers—each with distinct fuel implications. Guided by a carefully crafted reward function that penalizes collisions, excessive fuel usage, and risk, the RL module learns an optimal maneuver policy. 
39: Our evaluation demonstrates that this RL-based system significantly improves operational efficiency and decision-making. By intelligently balancing collision risk with fuel expenditure, the framework rigorously upholds satellite safety standards and provides a robust, autonomous alternative to conventional threshold-based methods.
40: Keywords: Autonomous Collision Avoidance, Reinforcement Learning (RL), Low Earth Orbit (LEO), Space Debris Mitigation, Markov Decision Process (MDP), Fuel Optimization, Satellite Safety

71: 1.1 Project Background
The Low Earth Orbit (LEO) environment is becoming increasingly congested due to a rapidly growing population of space debris, defunct satellites, and discarded rocket stages. This escalating volume of orbital debris introduces significant collision risks to critical satellite missions and global communication infrastructure. Every new piece of debris magnifies the probability of catastrophic collisions, which severely threatens operational space assets.  To overcome these limitations, this project introduces a novel, autonomous decision-support system powered by Reinforcement Learning (RL). The proposed system is designed to autonomously select collision avoidance maneuvers by evaluating complex conjunction events. By comprehensively modeling the environment with crucial parameters—such as collision probability, miss distance, relative velocity, and available fuel capacity—the RL agent is trained to identify and execute the most effective maneuvers. 
72: 1.2 Problem Statement
The fundamental challenge addressed in this project is the ability to safely navigate a spacecraft through a dynamic, hazard-filled environment—representing the debris-populated Low Earth Orbit (LEO)—while operating under strict resource limitations. The core problem is framed around a critical constraint: fuel is highly limited, and every maneuver incurs a tangible costWithin the context of our system simulation, this problem is defined by a dichotomy of choices that the agent must navigate:
73: Move: Executing an evasive maneuver successfully avoids immediate danger, but it fundamentally consumes irreplaceable fuel with every action taken. 
74: Stay (Idle): Choosing not to maneuver conserves fuel, but it significantly increases the risk of a collision with approaching debris or asteroids over time.

97: SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE
99: 4.1 High-Level Architecture
The proposed collision avoidance system is structured around a closed-loop Reinforcement Learning (RL) architecture, wherein the agent interacts with the environment in a continuous loop of perceiving, acting, and learning from rewards. This design enables the agent to continuously refine its navigation policy through experience, allowing it to adapt to dynamic threats while managing resources. 
100: The comprehensive architecture and data flow consist of four primary interconnected components: 
101: Simulation Environment: The environment is engineered as a 2D continuous space simulation that models the physical dynamics of the spacecraft. It incorporates velocity-based movement with applied friction, real-time rendering, and random asteroid spawning with variable speeds and directions, ensuring diverse and challenging conjunction scenarios. 
102: Lidar-Based Perception and Preprocessing: To perceive threats, the spacecraft relies on a 16-ray perception system that detects nearby obstacles in real time, generating a continuous observation range vector. This continuous data is fed into a state preprocessing and discretization module, which categorizes obstacle distances (e.g., Near, Medium, Far) to construct a structured state matrix for the neural network. 
103: RL Agent and Action Selection: The decision-making core is a Deep Q-Network (DQN) agent implemented using PyTorch. The agent processes the state matrix and employs an $\epsilon$-greedy policy to balance exploration and exploitation during action selection. It then outputs a specific maneuver command (such as moving Up, Down, Left, Right, or remaining Idle). 
104: Reward Evaluation and Policy Update: Following the execution of an action, the environment provides an immediate reward feedback loop—distributing positive reinforcement for survival and severe penalties for collisions. The agent utilizes this feedback to update its Q-values through the Bellman equation and temporal difference error calculations, learning the optimal survival strategy over successive episodes.

116: 5.2 Deep Q-Network Architecture
117: To effectively handle the complex, continuous state space presented in this problem, a Deep Q-Network (DQN) is employed to approximate the optimal action-value function, denoted as $Q(s, a; \theta)$, where $\theta$ represents the weights of the neural network.
118: The network architecture is structured as a fundamental fully connected deep neural network. The input layer consists of 35 neurons, directly corresponding to the 35-dimensional state vector (16 lidar distance values, 16 hit flags, normalized fuel level, and two velocity components). The internal processing is managed by multiple fully connected hidden layers utilized to learn the complex relationships between the state components. These hidden layers employ Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) nonlinear activation functions to capture non-linear dynamics. The output layer contains 5 neurons, with each neuron representing the predicted Q-value for one of the discrete actions in the action space (Idle, Thrust Up, Down, Left, and Right).
119: To enhance training stability and convergence, several critical mechanisms are integrated into the DQN architecture:
120: Experience Replay: The training process utilizes a reply buffer memory that stores transition tuples ($s, a, r, s', done$). During training, mini-batches of these transitions are sampled randomly. This technique effectively breaks the temporal correlation inherent in sequential observational data and improves data efficiency.
121: Target Network: A separate target neural network ($Q'$) is maintained specifically to compute stable target values ($y$). The weights of this target network are updated only periodically to match the primary online network. This prevents the primary network from chasing a rapidly moving target, which often leads to divergence in Deep RL. The target is calculated as:
123: Epsilon-Greedy Policy: To balance exploration of new strategies and exploitation of learned knowledge during training, an $\epsilon$-greedy action selection policy is implemented. This policy ensures that:With probability $\epsilon$, the agent selects a random action to explore the environment. Otherwise (with probability $1-\epsilon$), the agent exploits the current learned model by choosing $\arg\max Q(s, a)$.

126: 5.3 Reward Function
127: The reward function, $R_t$, is arguably the most critical component of the Reinforcement Learning framework, as it formalizes the system's objectives and shapes the agent's learned behavior. In this project, the reward function is meticulously designed to create a delicate balance between survival, active safety, and resource efficiency. It aims to train an agent that is not only risk-averse but also fuel-conscious.
128: The total reward accumulated at each time step $t$ is calculated as a composite sum of several distinct positive rewards and negative penalties:
130: The specific functional components of this summation are defined as follows:
131: Positive Rewards (Incentives):
132: Survival Reward ($R_{\text{survival}}$): A small, constant positive reward of +0.1 provided at every time step. This serves as baseline encouragement for the agent to simply stay alive as long as possible.
133: Safety Reward ($R_{\text{safety}}$): This is calculated as +0.3 $\times$ average lidar distance. It actively rewards the agent for maintaining a significant distance from all perceived obstacles, encouraging a defensive "cushion" around the spacecraft.
134: Goal Reward ($R_{\text{goal}}$): A substantial bonus of +1.0 awarded only when the agent successfully survives the entire targeted simulation duration. This is the terminal condition the agent aims to achieve.
135: Negative Rewards (Penalties):
136: Fuel Penalty ($R_{\text{fuel}}$): Calculated as $-0.02 \times \text{fuel used}$ during the time step. While necessary for maneuverability, excessive movement is penalized to encourage the agent to develop the optimal "think first" policy, thereby minimizing wasteful thrust actions.
137: Proximity Penalty ($R_{\text{proximity}}$): Calculated as $-0.1 \times \text{closeness to asteroids}$. Being close to an asteroid, even without a physical impact, is treated as risky behavior that is actively penalized.
138: Collision Penalty ($R_{\text{collision}}$): The most catastrophic outcome is assigned a massive negative reward of -1.0. 
139: Synthesis and Interpretation
140: By integrating these competing reward components, the agent is forced to navigate complex trade-offs. The combination of high collision and proximity penalties, against small survival rewards and a cost for fuel usage, leads to a reward structure that effectively ensures the agent learns to prioritize long-term survival through effective obstacle avoidance while concurrently developing strategies to use limited onboard fuel efficiently.

149: 6.1 Key Metrics
150: To rigorously evaluate the performance of the Deep Q-Network (DQN) agent and the effectiveness of the proposed collision avoidance framework, a set of key quantitative metrics was defined. These metrics assess the agent's capability across three critical dimensions: learning progress, operational safety, and resource efficiency. Tracking these metrics throughout the training process provides insight into the convergence of the model and its ability to generalize to new, stochastic obstacle scenarios.
151: The primary metrics used for evaluation are detailed below:
152: 1. Cumulative Episode Reward: This is the total reward accumulated by the agent over the course of a single episode. This metric serves as the comprehensive indicator of the agent's performance. As training progresses, an optimal learning curve should show this metric steadily increasing before asymptoting, indicating the agent is learning to maximize survival time and safety distance while minimizing fuel consumption and, most importantly, eliminating collision penalties.
153: 2. Average Episode Length (Survival Time): This measures the number of time steps (seconds in simulation time) the spacecraft survives in an episode without a collision. The objective is to maximize this time, eventually reaching the predetermined "goal time" (set to 500 steps in our simulation), at which point a terminal bonus reward ($R_{\text{goal}}$) is granted. An increasing average episode length demonstrates successful obstacle avoidance learning.
154: 3. Fuel Consumption: This is calculated as the cumulative total of fuel used during an episode based on the number of non-idle thrust actions taken. Monitoring fuel consumption is essential to verify that the agent is learning the intended "optimal resource management" policy. The goal is to observe a distinct decrease in total fuel usage once the agent stabilizes its survival strategies, proving it has learned to prioritize efficient maneuvering.
155: 4. Total Collisions: This tracks the raw number of catastrophic failure events. While the collision penalty ($R_{\text{collision}}$) in the reward function drives learning, this metric provides a direct, non-mathematical measurement of safety. In the evaluation phase, this metric should be near zero for a successful, deployment-ready agent.
156: 5. Average Lidar Distance: A metric specifically tracking the effectiveness of the active safety reward ($R_{\text{safety}}$). It measures the average separation distance maintained by the agent between the spacecraft and surrounding obstacles. A higher average value indicates that the agent is proactively keeping clear of obstacles rather than relying solely on last-minute maneuvers, which reduces proximity penalties.
158: 6.2 Convergence & Test Results
159: This section details the results obtained from training the Deep Q-Network (DQN) agent within the custom-built simulation environment and evaluates its performance during subsequent testing phases.
160: Training and Convergence Analysis
161: The DQN agent was trained over several thousand episodes to ensure robust learning in the stochastic asteroid-populated environment. A simulation goal time was set at 500 steps, meaning the agent had to survive for a predetermined duration to receive the terminal goal reward ($R_{\text{goal}}$).
162: The training progress, visualized through the metrics defined in Section 6.1, exhibited clear convergence trends that validated the reward function design and neural network architecture:
163: Cumulative Episode Reward: During the initial exploration phase (high $\epsilon$), the cumulative reward fluctuated significantly, often entering negative territory due to high penalties from frequent collisions and excessive fuel consumption. As training progressed and the exploration probability decayed, the reward curve showed a steady upwards trend, indicating the agent was learning to maximize positive reinforcements by prioritizing survival and distance from obstacles while actively minimizing costly fuel actions. The curve eventually asymptoted, suggesting the agent had successfully converged on an optimal policy.
164: Episode Length (Survival Time): Early in training, average survival time was low, as the agent had not yet learned to effectively utilize thrust to avoid spawning asteroids. However, a distinctive learning curve emerged: average episode lengths rapidly increased before stabilizing at the 500-step target. Following sufficient training, the agent was consistently capable of surviving the entire designated simulation duration by executing preventative rather than purely reactive maneuvers.
165: Fuel Consumption Behavior: A critical observation confirming the success of the "think first" objective was the evolution of fuel usage. Initially, the agent used high levels of fuel through inefficient, chaotic thruster firing. As learning stabilized, fuel consumption per episode dropped sharply and stabilized. This proved that the agent had internalized the fuel penalty ($R_{\text{fuel}}$) and learned to conserve resources by selecting optimal maneuvers (including the 'Idle' action) without compromising safety.
166: Testing and Evaluation Results
167: Following training convergence, the optimal model weights were frozen, and the trained agent was subjected to evaluation testing within the simulation. To rigorously test generalization, evaluation episodes utilized completely randomized asteroid spawning patterns and velocities that the agent had not encountered during training. The agent was required to navigate solely on its learned policy with exploration ($\epsilon$) set to zero.
168: The testing results were highly positive:
169: Obstacle Avoidance Performance: The agent successfully avoided near-miss and catastrophic collision scenarios with high reliability, consistently reaching the 500-step goal duration in randomized testing episodes. This demonstrated a strong capability to generalize learned avoidance patterns to new conjunction configurations.
170: Operational Efficiency: In comparison to earlier phases of training, the agent demonstrated a significantly refined fuel strategy, proving that the autonomous system learns a robust, fuel-efficient maneuver policy that dynamically balances risk and operational costs.
171: Qualitative Analysis: Observations of simulation playback indicated that the agent developed a defensive navigation behavior—proactively steering to maintain wide berths around obstacles rather than waiting until the last second, effectively minimizing the proximity penalty and maximizing the safety distance reward.

177: CONCLUSION
179: This project successfully designed and implemented an intelligent autonomous decision-support system for satellite collision avoidance in Low Earth Orbit (LEO). By modeling the problem as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) and utilizing a Deep Q-Network (DQN), the system demonstrated a robust capability to navigate a spacecraft through a dynamic, obstacle-filled environment while operating under strict fuel constraints.
180: The key contribution of this work lies in the formulation of a complex, multi-objective reward function. By intricately balancing competing priorities—maximizing survival and active safety measures against necessary fuel usage penalties—the Reinforcement Learning agent effectively learned an optimal policy that "thinks first." The agent successfully internalized safe navigation strategies, understanding that while immediate movement avoids a proximity risk, conserving the limited fuel is essential for long-term survival in the simulation. This adaptive, machine-learning-based approach overcomes the rigid limitations of traditional fixed, threshold-based collision avoidance methods.
181: In conclusion, this project provides a rigorous foundation for future automated space traffic management systems. The results prove that intelligent, autonomous decision-making can dramatically improve operational efficiency, strictly uphold satellite safety standards, and extend the mission lifespan of vital space infrastructure.
